Your AI keeps letting you down.

Here's why.

I've watched techs spend an hour fighting Claude over a ticket summary that should've taken three minutes. The tool isn't broken. The approach is.

Field notes by Aubrey Ballard — Sr. Systems Analyst · ~9 min · interactive

Here's a scene I lived: a tech on my team spent about 45 minutes bouncing back and forth with Claude trying to get a decent client-facing summary for a disk space ticket. The responses kept being either too technical, too vague, or just flat wrong about what we actually did. He was frustrated. I looked at the prompt. It said: "Summarize this ticket for the client."

That's it. No context about the client. No context about what tone we use with them. No context about which parts of the resolution actually mattered to a non-technical office manager versus the ten things we tried that didn't work. Claude had nothing. So it filled in the blanks — and it filled them in wrong.

I've seen this pattern repeat constantly — across our team, and from every tech I talk to who's trying to make AI actually work. Good techs, genuinely trying to use AI as a force multiplier, walking away from it thinking the tool doesn't work. It does work. But there's a gap between what's in your head and what ends up in the prompt — and AI cannot cross that gap on its own. It doesn't know what it doesn't know.

Exhibit A — The Context Gap
What's in your head

Write a runbook for resetting MFA on a locked M365 account, for a first-week Tier 1 working solo at 8pm, with the client on the phone, in our existing format, including what to check before escalating.

What the model gets

Write a runbook for resetting MFA on a locked M365 account, for a first-week Tier 1 working solo at 8pm, with the client on the phone, in our existing format, including what to check before escalating.

Everything in the bars never made it into the prompt. The model filled it in anyway — with someone else's assumptions. Tap the bars. That's the whole article.

"Every assumption AI makes on your behalf is a chance to drift from what you actually wanted."

— Something I say too often in our team standups

I came across a framework from Andrej Karpathy — the AI researcher who ran the AI team at Tesla and was part of the founding team at OpenAI — that gave me a cleaner way to think about this problem. He breaks it into three layers. I've been pressure-testing these layers against real MSP work for months. Here's what that looks like when your stack is M365, Meraki, and Azure, and your clients aren't patient.

01

Stop giving AI tasks. Give it goals.

// close_the_context_gap()

A task is what you want done. A goal is why you want it done and what a good result actually looks like. That distinction sounds obvious, but almost nobody carries it into their prompts. I didn't for a long time.

When I write a ticket runbook, I'm not just documenting steps. I'm writing something a Tier 1 tech can follow at 8pm on a weekend when I'm not available and the client is on the phone. The audience matters. The stakes matter. The moment the tech gets stuck matters. None of that lives in "write a runbook for resetting MFA."

a ticket summary. every msp has had this exact moment.

Same information. Completely different output. The only thing that changed was I told it who I was writing for and what success looked like.

The interview move

For anything bigger than a quick summary — a project, a deployment plan, a client-facing doc — I've started having Claude interview me before it does anything. I just say: "Before you start, ask me one question at a time to understand the actual goal here. Don't begin until you've got it."

It sounds like extra work. It isn't. It forces me to articulate things I'm holding in my head that I'd otherwise assume were obvious. Half the time I realize the goal I thought I had wasn't the real goal. Five minutes of questions saves thirty minutes of revision.

Agile over waterfall — every time

The other thing that kills AI-assisted work is handing over a big task and waiting for the finished result. Waterfall prompting fails the same way waterfall projects fail: the cost of a bad assumption grows with every step built on top of it. I've had Claude write entire runbooks that were technically accurate and completely wrong for our environment — because an assumption it made in step three was load-bearing by step fourteen. The output looks complete. It passes a glance. The damage doesn't surface until someone follows it.

The fix is checkpoints — the same discipline we'd never let a client skip. Break the work into stages and review each one before the next starts. When Claude drifts, you catch it at the stage boundary, where the fix costs a sentence. Catch it at the end and the fix costs the deliverable. It's the same reason we don't do a full network overhaul in one change window. Small chunks. Confirm the direction. Move forward.

Every automation and audit script I've shipped for clients started with a prompt that defined exactly what "done" looked like before a line of code got written. The ones that needed the most revision? Always the ones where I started with a vague ask and figured I'd know good when I saw it.

02

Define "done" before you start.

// verification_first()

The spec tells AI what to build. The verifier tells it how to know when it got it right. These are different things, and leaving the second one out is how you end up reviewing AI output by vibes.

I've learned to ask myself one question before I send any significant prompt: How will I know this is correct when I read it back? If the answer is "I'll just know," the prompt isn't ready yet. If I can't define done, Claude definitely can't.

2.1
Set the standard before you ask
What good looks like, in words
▶

Before Claude touches anything, write out your pass criteria. Not "looks professional" — something you could hand to a stranger and have them evaluate. "Three sections. Each ends with a concrete recommendation. No bullet points. Matches the format of last month's report. Under 400 words."

This isn't just for Claude. It forces you to be clear about what you actually want. Half the revision cycles I see — on my team and everywhere else — happen because the person prompting didn't know what they wanted until they saw something wrong.

2.2
Connect it to something real
External signal beats gut checks
▶

The strongest verifications I've run are the ones grounded in something concrete. If you're writing a client report, give Claude the structure and tone expectations up front — the section order, the voice, how we close — rather than leaving it to guess what a "professional report" looks like. Format and style guidance, not raw data.

When I had Claude help build the Azure Function proxy for our performance platform, I made it test against the live endpoint after each change instead of just confirming the code looked right. Catching issues at the deploy level instead of the review level saved real time.

2.3
Second pass from a fresh context
One model's blind spot is another's flag
▶

For things that really matter — client-facing docs, anything touching production, any code that runs unattended — I'll take the output and drop it into a fresh session with a different model or just a fresh Claude thread with no prior context.

Not to second-guess Claude. To see what stands out when there's no bias toward the original approach. "Here's a runbook. What's missing? What's ambiguous? What would a confused Tier 1 tech get stuck on?" Different framing, different surface area for problems.

The Feedback Loop

Boris Cherny — the person who built Claude Code — said that giving Claude a real feedback loop produces 2–3x better output. In our environment that means: don't just review the output yourself. Build the check into the process. Lint the code. Hit the endpoint. Compare to the reference. Make the verification automatic where you can.

A spec and a verifier will carry you a long way — on one piece of work. But notice what they have in common: you rebuild both from scratch every session. The spec you wrote yesterday is gone. The pass criteria you sharpened last week are sitting in a closed chat. The third layer exists to fix exactly that.

03

Build the workshop. Not just the work.

// environment_that_compounds()

Layers one and two help you get better results session by session. Layer three is about not starting from zero every time. Right now, most people open a new chat, dump in some context they're rebuilding from scratch, and call that their process. It works. It also means you're doing the same setup work indefinitely.

The persistent environment is what makes AI usage compound. Every bit of context you capture once and reuse is work you stop doing manually. Over a few months this gets significant.

CLAUDE.md — your standing brief

If you use Claude Code on a project, a file called CLAUDE.md at the repo root gets read automatically on every single prompt. This is where you put the stuff you'd otherwise have to explain again and again: how the repo is structured, what naming conventions we use, what the client's environment looks like, what you never want Claude to touch.

For any active project I keep the whole context in there — how the system is structured, what the naming conventions are, what the client's environment looks like, which pieces need a conversation before anyone touches them. One file. Every session starts already knowing all of that. You stop re-explaining and start building.

Guardrails — built into the environment, not the prompt

There's a difference between a rule and a request. Telling Claude "don't touch the auth flow" in a prompt is a request — it can still touch it if the reasoning feels compelling enough in context. Wiring a pre-tool-use hook that blocks writes to those files is a rule. The distinction matters when the cost of a mistake is high.

I think about this in three buckets. Assign everything in your project to one of them before you start any agentic work:

Always Do
Follow existing naming conventions
Comment new or modified logic
Flag assumptions — never fill silently
Checkpoint before continuing
Ask First
Delete or rename existing files
Touch config or environment files
Schema or architecture changes
Any operation you can't easily undo
Never Do
Touch protected folders
Push to production unsupervised
Modify auth or billing logic alone
Invent context — ask instead

Skills — turn repeated work into reusable process

Anything you find yourself rebuilding the same instructions for more than twice should become a skill file. Not an SOP in the traditional sense — more like a standing brief that tells Claude exactly how you do this specific type of work, in your shop, for your clients. Your runbook format. Your client email tone. The structure you use for incident reports.

The useful part isn't writing them once. It's that using them surfaces the gaps. Every time a skill produces something slightly off, that's a signal about something that wasn't precise enough in the instructions. Run it enough times and the skill gets sharp. The first version is never the final version.

The Compound Effect

I've been building this out for a few months now. The ROI isn't dramatic in week one. But six months in, every new project starts with a richer context, better default behavior, and a shorter path from "I need this" to "it's done and correct." That gap keeps shrinking. That's the actual value.
