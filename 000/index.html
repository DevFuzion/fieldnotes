What goes in never comes back.

Read this first.

Before the frameworks. Before the prompt engineering. Before anything in Issue 001 — one question: should this data be in an AI chat at all?

Field notes by Aubrey Ballard — Sr. Systems Analyst · ~7 min · interactive

A while back I walked past a screen and stopped. One of our techs had a full ticket thread copied — fourteen replies deep, the client's name in every signature, a temp password sitting in the third message — and a free AI chat window open in the next tab. He was about to ask it to "summarize this for the client."

Good instinct. Right tool for the job. And one keystroke away from handing a client's data to a company nobody in that ticket had ever heard of.

He hadn't done anything wrong yet. That's the entire point of this issue: the moment before the paste is the only moment that matters. After it, there's nothing to fix — because there's no version of "undo" that reaches into someone else's infrastructure and pulls your data back out.

This is Issue 000, and it's numbered that way on purpose. Everything else in this series — the spec writing, the verification, the environment building — assumes you've already answered the question above. So we answer it first.

01

Where it actually goes.

// follow_the_data()

When you paste something into an AI tool you don't control, three things are true. None of them are comforting.

You can't verify what happens next. I'm not going to tell you what any specific tool does with your data today, because by the time you read this it may have changed. Retention policies, training policies, what's covered on a free account versus a paid one — these vary by vendor, by tier, by a settings toggle you may never have seen. That's not paranoia. That's the actual reason you can't rely on it: the data handling of a tool you don't control is, by definition, unverifiable by you.

There is no recall. Every other mistake we make in this job has a remediation path. Wrong config? Roll it back. Deleted the wrong thing? Restore from backup. Pasted client data into a chat window? There is no ticket you can open for that. The data changed custody the moment you hit send, and the new custodian doesn't work for you.

It was never your data. This is the part that's specific to our job. An MSP tech sits inside dozens of client environments. Your chat history with an unmanaged AI tool isn't just your exposure — it's a cross-client data store accumulating one paste at a time. Our entire business is clients trusting us inside their walls. That trust doesn't have a clause for "but the summary was really good."

Some of those clients are healthcare — and healthcare is the clearest example because the obligations are written down and have a name: HIPAA. Patient information in a ticket doesn't stop being protected health information because it's on its way to a chatbot. But don't let the clearest example narrow the principle. Every client has data they'd expect us to treat exactly that carefully, whether the rules come from a regulator, a SOC 2 report, a contract, or just the handshake that won us the account.

What the tech saw: a routine ticket.
RE: RE: RE: Printer issues — Maria at the front desk called again, still can't print from the EHR. I reset her password to Sunrise2024! as a temp. Server is 10.1.40.12 if you need to RDP in. — Lakeside Family Clinic helpdesk thread
5 items left the building in one paste.

A staff member's identity. A live credential. An internal address. A client name. A system that handles patient data. None of it was the question — all of it was in the paste. Tap the ticket.

That ticket is fictional, but I've seen its cousins a dozen times. Nobody pastes credentials into an AI on purpose. They paste context — and the credentials ride along, because the whole thread was faster to grab than the relevant part.

02

The hard rules.

// the_never_paste_list()

Everything above leads to a short list. I'm not asking you to memorize a policy — these are conclusions. If you followed the logic this far, you've already written this list yourself.

And none of these come with a productivity cost, because every one of them has a substitute that works just as well. The model doesn't need the real thing. It needs the shape of the thing.

Never paste
Say instead
Credentials & secretsPasswords, API keys, MFA seeds, recovery codes — even expired ones, even "just to ask about the format."
The generic version"A service account password" or "an API key" tells the model everything it needs.
PeopleNames, emails, phone numbers, and anything resembling patient information — ever.
Roles, not names"The office manager." "A staff member." "A patient." The model genuinely does not care who.
Client identityCompany names, domains, and combinations of details that identify a client even without the name.
The anonymous profile"A 40-seat medical office." "A multi-site retail client." Specific enough to get a good answer.
Network internalsReal IPs, hostnames, topology, firewall rules, VPN configs.
Sanitized placeholders"10.x.x.x," "the file server," "a rule between the LAN and DMZ zones." Structure without coordinates.
Security findingsVulnerabilities, audit results, what's actually broken or exposed in a client's environment.
The class of issueAsk about the category — "handling an exposed SMB share" — not your client's instance of it.
Anything coveredData under HIPAA, SOC 2, or a contract. If you don't know whether it's covered —
It's coveredTreat every client like the obligations apply. The ones where they legally do won't be labeled for you.
The Pattern

Notice what every row has in common: the safe version is almost never a worse prompt. Half the time it's a better one — because stripping the specifics forces you to describe the actual problem, which is exactly the discipline Issue 001 is about.

03

The grey areas.

// run_the_breach_test()

The hard list covers the obvious. The grey areas are where judgment lives — the half-sanitized log, the error message with a hostname buried in line 40, the config snippet that's probably fine. You will hit these daily, and no list anyone writes will cover them all.

So here's the one question I run on everything that isn't obviously on the list:

"If this chat showed up in a breach dump tomorrow, would anyone need to be told?"

— The breach test. Run it before every send.

A client. Your manager. A regulator. If the honest answer is anyone at all — it doesn't go in. Rewrite it until the answer is no one.

The reason this works is that it skips the question you can't answer — "what does this tool actually do with my data?" — and asks the one you can: what happens if it escapes? That's the same question incident response asks, and it cuts through every grey area I've hit. A log with a hostname in it fails the test. The same log with the hostname swapped for "the file server" passes. Now you know exactly what to fix before you send.

Describe the shape, not the instance

The companion habit: you're almost never asking AI about your specific case. You're asking about the class of problem your case belongs to. "A Windows server is low on disk and I suspect profile bloat" gets you the same answer as pasting the real server's directory listing — without exporting anything. Train yourself to describe the shape. It's faster than sanitizing after the fact, and it makes you a better troubleshooter, because naming the class of problem is half of solving it.

When you genuinely can't tell

That's not a you-problem to solve alone at 8pm. Your shop almost certainly has an internal AI policy — go read it, because it exists for exactly this moment and it's the authority on the specifics, not this article. If something's still grey after that, ask whoever's senior. Thirty seconds of asking beats any amount of cleanup, of which — remember — there is none.

The Managed Exception

If your MSP has rolled out approved AI tooling with real data agreements behind it, the calculus changes — that's exactly what those deployments are for, and your internal policy will say what's in scope. This article is about everything else: the personal account, the free tier, the tool nobody vetted. When in doubt, assume that's what you're holding.
